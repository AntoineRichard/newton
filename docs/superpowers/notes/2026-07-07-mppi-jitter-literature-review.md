# MPPI Jitter Mitigation — Literature Review (deep research, 2026-07-07)

101-agent research harness: 5 search angles, top sources fetched, 3-vote adversarial
verification per claim. Verbatim verified output below.

## Summary

The literature converges on a clear diagnosis: MPPI's straight-line steering jitter arises primarily because standard MPPI injects i.i.d. per-timestep Gaussian noise, which overrepresents high-frequency components that vehicle dynamics dampen — so they contribute nothing to exploration or cost differentiation but survive the softmax average as chatter on the executed control. The strongest-evidenced and most practical remedies fall into four families: (1) control parameterization — sampling a small number of knots/control points and spline- or kernel-interpolating them (SCP-MPPI, CubicSpline-k4, KMPPI/SMPPI in pytorch_mppi), which is the best-supported mechanism the target system has not tried; (2) sampling-distribution shaping beyond AR(1) — frequency-domain 1/f^gamma colored noise, explicit low-pass filtering of noise perturbations before rollout (LP-MPPI), normal-log-normal mixtures (log-MPPI) that allow >30% lower injected variance, and Halton low-discrepancy sampling; (3) update-rule changes — Tsallis-divergence deformed-exponential weighting, which interpolates continuously between MPPI's soft averaging (the suspected mechanism for symmetric-sample cancellation jitter on cost-flat straights) and CEM's hard elite selection; and (4) a near-free trick from RA-MPPI — replacing a fraction eta≈0.2 of samples with zero-mean controls (u=epsilon rather than v+epsilon), explicitly reported as a smoothing mechanism. For the 1/10-scale RC car with 1024 rollouts at 30-50 Hz, the recommended priority order is: knot-based spline/kernel parameterization (highest evidence, moderate cost, directly reduces sampling dimension from H×m to K×m), the RA-MPPI zero-mean fraction (trivial cost), Tsallis/elite weighting to counter softmax averaging on straights, then LP-MPPI-style noise filtering or log-MPPI NLN sampling as alternatives to the existing AR(1) noise.

## Verified findings

### 1. Root cause consensus: standard MPPI samples control noise independently at each timestep, producing temporally uncorrelated, high-frequency-dominated control samples whose softmax average chatters. The high-frequency components are dampened by robot dynamics, so they barely affect rollout costs (no exploration benefit) yet cause actuator chattering and wear — this is the field's accepted explanation for MPPI jitter, including straight-line steering oscillation where cost gradients are flat.

- **Confidence:** high — merged from claims 0, 2, 10, 16, 18 (five independent primary papers; four unanimous 3-0, one 2-1)
- **Evidence:** Five independent primary sources state the same mechanism: 'sampling a Gaussian at every time step leads to control trajectories samples containing high-frequency noise ... chatter significantly. This can cause damage over time when applied to real systems' (2404.03094); 'robots dampen these components significantly, so they do not significantly affect obtained costs. Thus, they do not contribute to efficient exploration ... but instead result in chattering of applied controls and wear out of actuators' (2503.11717); 'Standard MPPI implementations typically sample control inputs independently at each time step, which can lead to temporally uncorrelated and non-smooth control sequences' (2601.01409). Note: no surviving claim directly confirmed the cost-flat-degeneracy-on-straights hypothesis; the literature attributes jitter to the sampling process itself, which explains why jitter persists even with rate costs.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/html/2404.10395v1, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.01409, https://arxiv.org/html/2404.03094v2, https://arxiv.org/html/2503.11717v1, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.03893

### 2. Control parameterization via sparse knots/control points with spline or kernel interpolation is the best-evidenced untried mechanism: sampling K control points instead of H timesteps and interpolating (cubic spline gives C2 continuity, reduces sampling dimension from H×m to K×m) yields smooth executed controls even under large sampling noise, without post-filtering. Empirically CubicSpline-k4 beat Gaussian iid, Bezier, and linear interpolation (100% success, fastest convergence, minimal variance) on locomotion; ready-made implementations exist as SMPPI and KMPPI (RBF kernel over support points, applied to the control signal not the state) in the UM-ARM-Lab pytorch_mppi library.

- **Confidence:** high — merged from claims 1, 3, 4, 5, 6 (all 3-0; three primary sources including one with quantitative ablation)
- **Evidence:** SCP-MPPI embeds 'spline interpolation within the MPPI process, enabling the generation of smooth control input sequences despite the utilization of substantial sampling noises' (samples ~4 control points vs ~150 timesteps). The legged-locomotion sampling-strategy study (2601.01409) found 'CubicSpline-k4 achieves a 100% success rate and converges significantly faster than all other methods' and offers the best balance of convergence, robustness, and compute. pytorch_mppi implements both SMPPI and KMPPI with configurable num_support_pts and TimeKernel (RBF, sigma), interpolating 'the control signal, not the state signal'. Caveat: smoothness benchmarks are on navigation/locomotion, not high-speed racing, so knot spacing vs replan rate (30-50 Hz) needs tuning to preserve corner-entry responsiveness.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/html/2404.10395v1, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.01409, https://github.com/UM-ARM-Lab/pytorch_mppi

### 3. RA-MPPI's zero-mean sample fraction is a trivial-cost, explicitly-for-smoothing technique: inject a fraction eta of sampled control sequences as pure zero-mean noise (u_k = epsilon_k) rather than noise around the nominal (u_k = v_k + epsilon_k); RA-MPPI uses eta = 0.2. On a straight where the optimal steering is near zero, these samples pull the softmax average back toward zero and damp accumulated nominal drift.

- **Confidence:** high — claim 23, 3-0 (peer-reviewed IEEE paper by Theodorou/Tsiotras group; technique traces to original Williams MPPI work)
- **Evidence:** 'Line 8 produces sampled controls by adding control noise to mean controls. Line 10 introduces some zero-mean controls to help smooth the resulting controls [11].' Verified eta = 0.2 in their experimental setup. Implementation cost is a few lines (mask 20% of the 1024 rollouts); directly compatible with existing AR(1) noise and rate costs.
- **Sources:** https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10499424

### 4. Tsallis-divergence weighting generalizes MPPI's update rule: replacing exp(-J/lambda) with the deformed exponential exp_r(-J/lambda) unifies MPPI, CEM, VI-MPC, and Stein VI-MPC; as r grows the weight becomes a hard threshold 1{J<=gamma} that zeros out non-elite rollouts (CEM-style elite selection) instead of soft exponential averaging over all samples. This directly targets the softmax-averaging failure mode where near-symmetric samples on a cost-flat straight all receive similar weights and their noise averages into the nominal.

- **Confidence:** high — merged from claims 21, 22 (both 3-0; RSS 2021 paper from the original MPPI group)
- **Evidence:** The paper 'includes prior works such as Variational Inference-Model Predictive Control, Model Predictive Path Integral Control, Cross Entropy Method, and Stein Variational Inference Model Predictive Control as special cases' and shows 'As r->inf, exp_r(-lambda^-1 J) -> the step function 1{J<=gamma}'. A finite tuned r gives a middle ground between soft averaging and hard elite selection. Implementation cost: change one line in the weight computation plus tune r. Caveat: the paper demonstrates the unification and performance, not specifically straight-line jitter reduction — that link is inferential.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.00241

### 5. LP-MPPI applies a low-pass filter directly to the sampled noise perturbations before rollout (not to the nominal control), removing the high-frequency components that cause chattering while retaining low-frequency exploration. This is a sharper-cutoff alternative to AR(1) noise (AR(1) is a first-order 6 dB/octave filter; LP-MPPI's configurable filter order o_LPF and cutoff f_c can suppress the jitter band far more aggressively).

- **Confidence:** high — claim 15 (+16), 3-0 (2025 primary paper)
- **Evidence:** Algorithm 2: 'Filter noise sequence epsilon_i^LP = LPFilter(epsilon_i, f_c, o_LPF) using low-pass filter', then U_i = U + epsilon_i^LP. The paper's rationale: white-noise samples overrepresent high frequencies that dynamics dampen, so filtering them 'before simulating trajectories' reduces actuator chattering without hurting cost-relevant exploration. For the RC car this is a drop-in replacement for the AR(1) noise generator with a steeper spectral rolloff.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/html/2503.11717v1

### 6. Frequency-domain colored-noise sampling (Vlahov/Theodorou et al.) generates noise with PSD proportional to 1/f^gamma via inverse FFT, integrating with only minimal changes to MPPI's update rules; gamma tunes smoothness independently of variance. This subsumes AR(1) as a special case but allows steeper spectra. However, the stronger claim that colored noise is simultaneously more reactive AND less oscillatory than Gaussian was refuted in verification (1-2 vote) — the literature reports a smoothness-vs-exploration trade-off, not a free lunch.

- **Confidence:** high — claim 11, 3-0 (mechanism); companion reactivity claim refuted 1-2
- **Evidence:** 'We show how a frequency-based sampling distribution can be used in MPPI with minimal adjustments to the update rules ... Colored Noise distributions cover a large space of distributions that have PSD of the form PSD(f) proportional to 1/f^gamma'. Samples remain Gaussian in the time domain; the iFFT is subsumed into the cost so the same update law applies. Since the target system already uses AR(1), the incremental value is the tunable gamma exponent, i.e. lower marginal benefit than the untried mechanisms above.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/html/2404.03094v2

### 7. log-MPPI reshapes the sampling distribution itself: control-input updates are drawn from a normal-log-normal (NLN) mixture (product of a normal and a log-normal) instead of a Gaussian. This lets a >30% smaller injected noise variance produce equally well-spread rollouts, avoiding both constraint violation from large variance and sample concentration in high-cost regions from small variance — attacking jitter at its source (injected variance) without sacrificing exploration.

- **Confidence:** high — merged from claims 7, 8, 9 (all 3-0; peer-reviewed IEEE RA-L 2022 with public ROS implementation, actively extended by follow-on work)
- **Evidence:** 'the trajectories (or, the control input updates) are sampled from the product of normal and log-normal distribution (namely, NLN mixture), rather than from Gaussian distribution'; 'a small injected noise variance can be utilized so that violating system constraints can be avoided; yet, we can still get desirable sampled rollout trajectories that are well spread-out'; abstract reports 'better exploration of the state-space ... with more than 30% reduction in the injected noise variance'. Caveat: demonstrated on 2D AGV occupancy-grid navigation, not racing; the >30% figure is self-reported.
- **Sources:** https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.16599

### 8. Low-discrepancy and deterministic sampling reduce jitter by improving sample coverage: Halton-MPPI replaces pseudo-random Gaussian draws with a Halton sequence for spatial coverage, combined with an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process for temporal correlation (OU is the continuous-time analog of AR(1)); dsMPPI goes further, replacing random sampling entirely with deterministic samples while keeping MPPI's exponential weighting, and self-reports smoother trajectories than state-of-the-art baselines. C-Uniform sampling shapes control-input probabilities so trajectories cover configuration space uniformly, though it targets exploration rather than smoothness.

- **Confidence:** medium — merged from claims 12, 13, 14 (3-0, MDPI Drones venue), 18/19/20 (3-0/3-0/2-1, single Jan 2026 preprint, self-reported benchmarks), 17 (3-0, mechanism-only)
- **Evidence:** Halton-MPPI: 'utilizes the Halton sequence as the sampling basis for control disturbances to enhance spatial coverage, while the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process is introduced to impose temporal correlation'; identifies 'pseudo-random Gaussian sampling ... non-uniform sample distributions and jitter-prone control sequences' as the problem. dsMPPI 'leverages the exponential weighting of MPPI alongside the efficiency of deterministic samples' and 'achieves smoother trajectories compared to state-of-the-art methods' (self-reported, unspecified baselines). C-Uniform 'allows the computation of control input probabilities to generate trajectories that sample the configuration space uniformly'. Confidence lowered because: MDPI mid-tier venue, dsMPPI single unreviewed preprint with one 2-1 vote, and the OU component duplicates the user's existing AR(1) noise.
- **Sources:** https://www.mdpi.com/2504-446X/10/2/96, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.03893, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.05819

### 9. Ranked recommendation for the RC-car stack (already has AR(1) noise, rate costs, exec-coupling), by evidence strength × implementation cost: (1) RA-MPPI zero-mean sample fraction eta≈0.2 — trivial, high evidence; (2) knot/spline or kernel control parameterization (K≈4-8 knots over the horizon, reference implementation in pytorch_mppi KMPPI) — highest evidence for smoothness, moderate cost, also cuts effective sampling dimension for the 1024 rollouts; (3) Tsallis/deformed-exponential weighting or a hard elite threshold — one-line weight change targeting softmax averaging on cost-flat straights; (4) LP-MPPI higher-order low-pass on noise perturbations — steeper spectral rolloff than existing AR(1); (5) log-MPPI NLN sampling to cut injected variance ~30%; (6) Halton/deterministic sampling — weakest incremental evidence given AR(1)/OU overlap.

- **Confidence:** medium — synthesis judgment over the verified findings, not an independently verified claim
- **Evidence:** Ranking derived from: (a) parameterization has three independent primary sources including a controlled ablation (CubicSpline-k4 winning) and off-the-shelf code; (b) zero-mean fraction is explicitly labeled a smoothing mechanism in a peer-reviewed paper and costs a few lines; (c) Tsallis directly modifies the averaging step implicated in symmetric-sample cancellation; (d) noise-spectrum methods (LP-MPPI, colored, NLN, Halton+OU) are partially redundant with the existing AR(1) noise, so expected marginal gain is lower. All smoothness mechanisms report a smoothness-vs-responsiveness trade-off; none claims to eliminate it (the one claim asserting no trade-off was refuted in verification).
- **Sources:** https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10499424, https://github.com/UM-ARM-Lab/pytorch_mppi, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.01409, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.00241, https://arxiv.org/html/2503.11717v1, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.16599

## Caveats

Three gaps in the surviving evidence. (1) Domain transfer: none of the verified sources evaluates on high-speed autonomous racing with straight-line steering oscillation specifically — evidence comes from ground-vehicle navigation, quadruped locomotion, AGVs, and drones; the ranking's applicability to a 30-50 Hz RC-car racing loop is an informed extrapolation. (2) The hypothesized WHY-mechanisms specific to straights — cost-flat direction degeneracy, softmax averaging of symmetric samples, noise accumulation under saturation — did not survive verification as directly-sourced claims; the literature's verified explanation is the more general i.i.d./high-frequency-noise mechanism, so the Tsallis recommendation for straight-line jitter rests on inference. (3) Several sources are single-paper or self-reported: dsMPPI (unreviewed Jan 2026 preprint, one 2-1 vote, self-selected baselines), Halton-MPPI (MDPI mid-tier venue, motivational framing), log-MPPI's >30% variance figure (self-reported on 2D navigation). The refuted claim that colored noise gives smoothness without a responsiveness cost is a warning: expect every mechanism here to trade some corner-entry reactivity for straight-line smoothness. Notably absent from surviving claims: iCEM elite smoothing/reuse, DIAL-MPC, Tube-MPPI, Savitzky-Golay post-filtering, covariance adaptation, and AutoRally-specific practices — the survey question asked about them but no claims about them survived verification, so their absence reflects verification attrition, not evidence they are ineffective. Time-sensitivity: several sources are very recent (2025-2026 preprints) and may change under review.

## Open questions

- Does the cost-flat-degeneracy/softmax-averaging explanation for straight-line-specific jitter hold empirically — e.g., does elite-only (Tsallis r large or CEM-style) weighting measurably reduce straight-line oscillation relative to soft averaging at equal rollout budget on a racing task?
- What knot count / support-point spacing preserves corner-entry responsiveness at 30-50 Hz replan on a 1/10-scale car — is K=4 over a ~1-2 s horizon too sluggish for late-apex corrections, and how should knots be warm-shifted between replans?
- How do iCEM-style elite reuse, DIAL-MPC, Tube-MPPI, and gradient-augmented MPPI variants compare on smoothness — none survived verification, so their relative merit against the mechanisms above is unmeasured?
- Do the smoothing mechanisms compose — e.g., does spline parameterization plus zero-mean sample fraction plus existing rate costs over-damp the controller, and is there published guidance on combining rather than choosing among them?
