# Wheeled Vehicle Phase 5 Newton Collision Terrain Contact Spec

## Purpose

Phase 5 validates the Newton-contact patch path on terrain that is not just the
flat reference ground plane. It consumes the Phase 1A wheel tables, Phase 1B
`WheelContactPatchState`, Phase 3 tire inputs, and Phase 4 vehicle command
mapping, then checks whether Newton collision contacts provide enough patch
geometry, normals, terrain identity, and material data for tire models on
representative terrain.

This phase remains a contact-quality and validation phase. It should not add a
raycast wheel path and should not jump ahead to hydroelastic contacts or a new
tire model. The goal is to learn whether the rigid contact cloud is good enough,
where it fails, and which setup knobs must be controlled for wheel-terrain
pairs.

## Goals

- Validate wheel contact patches on primitive and mesh terrain using Newton's
  collision pipeline.
- Make wheel-terrain contact setup explicit, including contact gap and margin
  handling for wheel shapes and terrain shapes.
- Confirm that cylindrical wheel geometry produces useful contact clouds for
  tires, or document when the manifold degenerates to a line or point.
- Determine whether any cylinder contact points are deliberately projected or
  aligned for rolling stabilization, and whether wheel-terrain pairs need a
  clean opt-out or alternate contact mode.
- Preserve material and terrain shape data so `WheelContactPatchState` can seed
  tire friction and diagnostics on non-flat terrain.
- Measure jitter, sparse-contact cases, normal stability, patch area stability,
  and multi-world performance.
- Compare Newton-generated contacts converted into MuJoCo Warp against
  MuJoCo-native contacts only as an observability/stability study.

## Non-Goals

- Do not add raycast terrain contact.
- Do not implement hydroelastic contacts; Phase 6 studies that separately.
- Do not replace the saturated-linear tire model with Brush, Fiala, or Pacejka.
- Do not add motor curves, differentials, or powertrain modules.
- Do not change Phase 1A wheel metadata or fold terrain fields into wheel
  metadata.
- Do not make wheel spin physical in the tire path.
- Do not replace the cylinder wheel fixtures unless Phase 5 measurements show a
  concrete collision-quality problem that cannot be handled by setup or patch
  estimation.

## Existing Handoff

Phase 5 starts from these public/internal pieces:

- `newton.wheeled.WheeledModelMetadata` for flat wheel identity, wheel shape,
  wheel body, radius, width, and vehicle id.
- `newton.wheeled.WheelContactPatchState` for wheel contact activity, contact
  count, terrain shape index, patch center, patch normal, extents, patch area,
  material friction seed, and normal force diagnostics.
- `newton.wheeled.WheelTireControl` and `WheelTireState` for tire inputs and
  tire diagnostics.
- `newton.wheeled.WheeledVehicleLayout` and `WheeledVehicleControl` for
  Ackermann and skid-steer command mapping.
- `Model.shape_gap`, `Model.shape_margin`, material friction arrays, and Newton
  collision contact buffers.

`WheelContactPatchState` already estimates extents from the rigid contact cloud.
Phase 5 should test and, only if needed, refine how those extents are interpreted
for cylinder tires and terrain contacts.

## Contact Gap And Margin Policy

Rigid contact gaps are controlled by `Model.shape_gap`, populated from
`ModelBuilder.ShapeConfig.gap` or the builder default rigid gap. MuJoCo geom gap
is already forced to `0.0` when Newton shape properties are copied into MuJoCo,
so the Phase 5 gap work is primarily about Newton collision contact generation.

For wheel-terrain validation scenes:

- Wheel shapes and terrain shapes should use contact gap `0.0` unless a specific
  test is sweeping gap values.
- Tests should record the actual `shape_gap` and `shape_margin` values used by
  wheel and terrain shapes before contact generation.
- Gap-zero tests should confirm contacts are generated by actual shallow
  penetration or near-surface support rather than by an inflated detection shell.
- Margin behavior should be studied separately from gap behavior. If margin is
  used for support-map contact robustness, tests should report it explicitly.

The working hypothesis from solver guidance is that setting the contact gap to
zero lets cylinder tires sink slightly into the floor, producing more useful
contact geometry and avoiding early, overly separated contacts. Phase 5 should
validate or reject that hypothesis with measurable patch diagnostics.

## Cylinder Tire Contact Quality

The Phase 00 fixtures use simple cylinder wheel collision geometry. On a flat
plane, the geometric ideal for a rigid cylinder is line contact across the tire
width. The tire model, however, consumes an estimated patch center, normal,
extents, and area. Phase 5 must verify what Newton actually produces for these
cylinder contacts.

For each representative wheel-terrain pair, tests should record:

- contact count per wheel;
- patch center and normal;
- patch extent along two tangent axes;
- patch area;
- ratio between the smaller and larger tangent extent;
- frame-to-frame variation in normal, center, extents, and area;
- whether the contact cloud is point-like, line-like, or area-like.

A line-like patch is not automatically a failure. It becomes a failure only when
it makes tire inputs unstable or unusable. If cylinder contacts collapse to a
line on flat terrain but remain stable, Phase 5 may keep the estimator and add a
well-documented area fallback later. If the contact cloud is sparse or jittery on
non-flat terrain, Phase 5 should identify whether the issue comes from contact
gap, margin, contact reduction, cylinder geometry, terrain tessellation, or
solver force reporting.

## Cylinder Contact Line Alignment Investigation

Phase 5 should explicitly audit whether line-like wheel patches come from the
expected geometry of cylinder-plane contact or from an implementation heuristic
that forces contact points onto a line.

Known places to inspect before changing behavior:

- the analytical plane-cylinder helper, which emits a fixed tripod in
  near-upright cases and rolling-mode side-generator contacts otherwise;
- the GJK/MPR manifold path for cylinder contacts against non-plane primitive or
  mesh terrain;
- the axial-shape rolling stabilization post-process, which can project a
  cylinder/cone contact point onto a rolling stabilization plane for
  axial-vs-discrete contacts;
- any deterministic contact reduction or sorting path that may collapse a
  wider contact cloud before `WheelContactPatchState` sees it.

If a forced line projection is confirmed and it makes wheel patch inputs worse,
the fix should be a clean, scoped contact-generation option. Candidate designs
include a per-shape or per-pair contact mode for wheel-terrain pairs, a
wheel-specific collision helper that bypasses axial rolling projection, or an
internal collision-pipeline option exposed through a public `newton.wheeled`
setup helper. The default behavior for general cylinder contacts should not
change without evidence that the broader collision pipeline benefits.

## Terrain Cases

Use a small deterministic set of terrain cases before adding large scenes:

- flat plane baseline with gap `0.0`;
- inclined plane or ramp;
- shallow curb or step;
- convex/primitive obstacle such as a low box ridge;
- triangle mesh terrain with a shallow bump, ripple, or jump profile.

Each case should run with the RC car and Husky fixtures where feasible. Tests may
use one vehicle at a time for clarity, but at least one multi-world test should
verify batching and replication.

## Patch Diagnostics And Acceptance

Phase 5 should keep diagnostics wheel-indexed and flat. If additional quality
metrics are needed, prefer tests or a small internal helper before adding public
API. Public fields should only be added if examples or downstream tire models
need them.

Minimum acceptance for terrain-contact quality:

- active wheel-terrain contacts are detected on all baseline terrain cases;
- contact normals point away from terrain support and remain finite;
- terrain shape indices and material friction seeds are populated;
- patch center and extents remain finite and deterministic enough for tests;
- cylinder wheel contacts are classified and documented as point-like,
  line-like, or area-like for each terrain case;
- gap `0.0` behavior is covered by tests and documented in the example setup;
- tire forces can consume the resulting patches without non-finite state.

## Solver And Contact Ownership

The wrapped rigid solver still owns normal contact support. The wheeled layer
owns wheel-specific longitudinal and lateral tire friction. Phase 5 should keep
normal-only MuJoCo wheel contact configuration for tire examples unless a test is
explicitly comparing friction ownership.

When comparing Newton-generated contacts and MuJoCo-native contacts, the output
should be a report or regression test about observability and stability. It
should not change the main architecture unless the comparison finds a concrete
blocking issue.
